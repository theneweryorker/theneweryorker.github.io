Section 2.

A long winded meta essay on the state of writing on the internet, and why I’m doing it.

I define “internet writing” as: Tweets, Substacks, Medium essays, Tumblr posts, captions on Instagram stories, public Discord conversations, and digital publications. In short, almost anything with a shareable link. In my observation, folks of a certain generation (Gen-Z/cusp Millennials) are often pressured — whether overtly by peers, or inadvertently through the perceived successes of others-not-abstained — to produce thoughts and ideas on a frequent basis online. We are promised the allure of followers (for brand partnerships or emotional buttressing) or thought-leadership (definitions here still elude me).

Herein lies the source of my perpetual angst: Not having a body of work to evince the existence or quality of one’s inner thoughts — or yet another take on whether or not AI will doom the creative class — can sometimes feels like indolence. After all, Twitter and Substack are free to all. If others can build prolific careers or rich communities from these platforms, why not you? Much has been written about how the FOMO of constant self-improvement is just another self-enforcement mechanic of late-stage capitalism — it tries to convince you that a lack of participation (read: a lack of public contribution) is irresponsible somehow, though you can’t exactly place why. This I understand, and still grapple with.

Of course, a lot happened to get us here. (Click on caret to expand.)

There was Tumblr, Twitter, and Medium, which — I’ll speak in general strokes here — standardized this kind of solipsistic and informal essay writing*. The essays were often shrouded in the lived experiences of the author; their intuited opinions and observations were inherently non-fact-checkable. Writing here could feel authoritative while being nearly entirely subjective, and could be readily produced. It was easy for both the author and the reader, requiring little fact-checking for either party, and was often instantly relatable and shareable.

Then there was the already-burgeoning preference for individuals over institutions when it came to consumption. (Out of growing distrust, or a trend of nichification and personalization of web behavior ?) You’d trust an influencer more than a faceless clothing corporation; your favorite editor instead of Man Repeller. It was fun to get your takes straight from the source, with no one in the middle.

One upside of the genre’s popularization was that it was accessible: suddenly anyone could weigh in on pressing issues of our time with no apparent authority; just existing and observing was prerogative enough to say something on the matter. The downside, however, was excess. Not just more essays, but, thanks to Substack, often essays on a weekly basis*.

Here's why this exists here today: This very writing is both a concession to and a deliberate attempt to investigate my beliefs apart from from the constant, mostly-performative thought-production business that has become the internet. In other words: yes, I’m adding more words to the world wide web, and I’m also deliberately stripping them of any internet currency so that I can write freely*: they will never make it to a Twitter feed, a Substack email. In fact, may you never read this unless you’ve organically found me.

To me, Jia Tolentino’s Trick Mirror is a perfect example of this (and is, admittedly, a very good read.) It encapsulated this specific solipsistic sensibility perfectly: sufficiently-researched cultural criticism with manageably abstract provocations, delivered in the first-person. In all its self-assuredness and informality, it read like a millennial diary entry if it was given a journalistic brief: acerbic sociocultural commentary sprinkled with tenets of nth-wave feminism and occasional qualitative data points, marked by the niche, conceptual vocabulary of new-age internet studies programs. To date, I have 523 unread Substack emails. writing freely: without the surveillance of an anticipated audience.
