Section 2.

A long winded meta essay on the state of writing on the internet in 2023, and why I’m doing it.

I define “internet writing” as: Tweets, Substacks, Medium essays, Tumblr posts, captions on Instagram stories, public Discord conversations, and digital publications. In short, almost anything with a shareable link. In my observation, folks of a certain generation (Gen-Z/cusp Millennials) are often pressured — whether overtly by peers, or inadvertently through the perceived successes of others-not-abstained — to produce writing (thoughts, ideas) on a frequent basis online. We are promised the allure of followers (for brand partnerships or emotional buttressing) or thought-leadership (definitions here elude me).

Personally, not having a body of work to evince the existence or quality of one’s inner thoughts — or yet another take on whether or not AI will doom the creative class — sometimes feels like indolence. After all, Twitter and Substack are free to all. If others can build prolific careers or rich communities from these platforms, why not you? Yes, the FOMO is probably just another self-enforcement mechanic of late-stage capitalism. You know the one: the one which tries to convince ourselves that a lack of participation — inherently a lack of public contribution — is irresponsible somehow, though we can’t exactly place why.

Of course, there were a lot of concurrent trends to get us here.

There was Tumblr, Twitter, and Medium, which — I’ll speak in general strokes here — standardized this kind of solipsistic and informal essay writing. The essays were often shrouded in the lived experiences of the author; their intuited opinions and observations were inherently non-fact-checkable. Writing here could feel authoritative while being nearly entirely subjective, and could be readily produced. It was easy for both the author and the reader, requiring little fact-checking for either party, and was often instantly relatable and shareable.

One upside of the genre’s popularization was that it was accessible: suddenly anyone could weigh in on pressing issues of our time with no apparent authority; just existing and observing was prerogative enough to say something on the matter. The downside, however, was excess. Not just more essays, but, thanks to Substack, often essays on a weekly basis.

This very writing is both a concession to and a deliberate attempt to investigate my beliefs apart from the constant, mostly-performative thought-production business that has become the internet. In other words: yes, I’m adding more words to the world wide web, and I’m also deliberately stripping them of any internet currency so that I can write freely*: they will never make it to a Twitter feed, a Substack email, and you’ll never read them unless you’ve organically found me.
