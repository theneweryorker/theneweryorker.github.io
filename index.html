Section 2.

A long winded meta essay on the state of writing on the internet, and why I’m doing it.

I saw the best writers of my generation destroyed by their venture capital CRM newsletters.

If you subscribe to the cohort of perennially online Gen-Z/Millennials, chances are you've felt pressured — whether overtly by peers, or inadvertently through the perceived successes of others-not-refrained — to produce internet writing on a frequent basis online. (I define “internet writing” as: Tweets, Substacks, Medium essays, Tumblr posts, captions on Instagram stories, public Discord conversations, and digital publications... almost anything with a shareable link or that can be screenshotted as a meme.) The promise? Niche digital fame or "thought-leadership," though definitions here still elude me.

Herein lies the source of my perpetual angst: to abstain or to succumb? Not having a body of work to evince the existence or quality of one’s inner thoughts — or yet another take on whether or not AI will doom the creative class — can sometimes feels like indolence, (especially in a tech job1). After all, Twitter and Substack are free to all. If others can build prolific careers or rich communities from these platforms, why not you? Much has been written about how the FOMO of constant self-improvement is just another self-enforcement mechanic of late-stage capitalism — it tries to convince you that a lack of participation (read: a lack of contribution or output) is irresponsible somehow, though you can’t exactly place why. This I understand, and yet still grapple with.

Of course, a lot happened to get us here. (Click caret to read more)

There was the era of Tumblr, Twitter and Medium which standardized a kind of especially-solipsistic and informal essay writing2. The essays were often couched in the lived experiences of the author and intuited opinions and observations were inherently non-fact-checkable. This style of writing felt authoritative while being nearly entirely subjective , and therefore could be readily produced. It was easy for both the author and the reader, as it required little fact-checking for either party and lent itself to being instantly relatable and shareable.

This was buoyed by the already-burgeoning preference for content from individuals over entities. (Out of a groundswell of institutional distrust? Or the larger trend of web nichification?) You’d trust an influencer more than a faceless clothing corporation; your favorite editor from Man Repeller instead of Man Repeller. It was exciting, empowering to hear from anyone — even somewhat well-known people— directly3, with no one in the middle.

One upside of the genre’s popularization was that it was accessible: suddenly anyone could weigh in on pressing issues of our time with no apparent authority; just existing and observing was prerogative enough to say something on the matter. The downside, however, was excess. Not just more essays, but, thanks to Substack, often essays on a weekly basis4. Over time, it feels like the global average of writing on the internet had been fundamentally altered: "I think" making up the largest share of our shared lexicon (and to which I've now contributed!) (See Fig 2.)

Welcome to the little peace I've made: This writing is both a concession to and a deliberate attempt to investigate my beliefs apart from the constant, mostly-performative thought-production business that has become the internet. I am adding more words to the world wide web. I’m also hoping that by deliberately stripping them of any internet currency, I can write freely5: I don't own an (active) Twitter account, and I promise never to make my friends endure a half-hearted Substack attempt. After all, there are always better things to read on the internet, like this.

AI and globalization: The drop-shipping of plastic and ideas
It seems to me that the most prominent anxieties surrounding AI (displacement of labor; cheapened quality of outputs) and its upside (heightened efficiency; anything you can imagine made real) echo the very arguments I heard growing up in the 90s and early 00s around globalization. In some ways, the way I felt using Stable Diffusion for the first time reminded me just a little of my first time walking into Forever21 in middle school: impossibly in awe of the variety that was possible. Not to draw too many parallels: both business models (fast fashion, and transformers) rely on pilfering from existing artists.
